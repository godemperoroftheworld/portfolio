### Trans Aesthetics and Creating “Something Else To Be”

Under contemporary regimes of control, heteropatriarchal scripts remain a pervasive, colonizing force– a “product and producer of carceral logics” that seek to relegate us into conformity, and a drive to “assimilation” that is “killing us”  (Bey 137; Cohen 445). By concealing “labour under the sign of nature”, these directives masquerade as absolute truths, concealing a precarious construction that requires constant enforcement (Ahmed 145). For the survival of queer subjects, rupturing from these norms is vital, propelling us toward “another way of being”– one that generates new “life narratives” and alternative queer “relations to time and space” (Malatino 124; Halberstam 13). This ideological struggle is viscerally mirrored in the language of modern cinema, where Hollywood’s rigid continuity and genre convention enforce a manufactured “normal”. Functioning as an aesthetic extension of the “petro-sexo-racial capitalist regime”, this “narrative construction of heteronormativity” falsely purports itself as “pure reality” (Preciado 19,37; Keegan 136). The films in this program violently reject this facade, employing distinct visual and structural dysfluency to break from dominant norms. Through their trans-gressive formal properties, _I Saw the TV Glow_, _The Matrix_, and _Love Lies Bleeding_ make visible the conventional script and its damage, breaking with it to embrace the “livable wreckage” and imagine “something else to be” (Bey 146; Fawaz 335).  

The centralized, heterosexist institutions dominating the colonial world function to “legitimize and privilege” cisheteronormativity as what is “fundamental” and “natural”, dictating our lives and “[shaping] what [..] is possible” for us to do (Cohen 440; Ahmed 145). However, this constructed reality is, in Edelman’s words, a “Ponzi scheme” in service of a future which never truly arrives (Edelman 4). This narrative must be constantly repeated, lest the vapid future it enshrines come apart. In _Love Lies Bleeding_, the repeated motifs of the gym, of exercise equipment, of guns– often in montage– formalize the repetition of this narrative. These masculinized, often phallic symbols of strength– big muscles, big trucks, big guns– are vital for patriarchal systems to project their illusory reality and vigor. ![](@assets/blog/breaking-the-script/image21.png)Far from natural, these “normalizing categories” are vital “instruments” for the “oppressive structures” of “regulatory regimes”– regimes that seek to colonize “our sexuality, exploit our labor, and constrain our visibility” (Butler 575; Cohen 440). In _The Matrix_, we see this formalized in mise-en-scène, as Neo gets a glimpse of humanity twisted into “living implants” violently “sculpted” to form “machinic grease for the systems in place that wish only for their own perpetuation” (Preciado 17; Bey 134).  
![](@assets/blog/breaking-the-script/image3.png)In this fractured and broken world, grimy props and locations contrast with the green-hued colour grading of Neo’s sterile, corporatized former existence, exposing normative reality as a fabricated prison. In _I Saw the TV Glow_, mise-en-scène serves an equally crucial role, producing a liminal world that never feels quite right; like a foggy, empty memory, it formalizes the dissociative nature of conforming to a world you don’t belong to, contrasting this with the neon warmth from the pink opaque.  
![](@assets/blog/breaking-the-script/image10.png)To maintain their pervasive control, regulatory norms trap subjects in– as Preciado describes– a rigid “binary epistemology” (Preciado 17). _The Matrix_ frequently presents Neo with rigid binaries: Neo can save himself, _or_ Morpheus. Neo can be a responsible programmer _or_ a nefarious hacker. Neo can take the red pill _or_ the blue pill. 
![](@assets/blog/breaking-the-script/image9.png)In this moment, reality is doubled, as Neo simultaneously does and doesn’t reach for a pill in a single shot, an impossibility that violates ordained cinematic reality. Indeed, this reality cannot exist without enforcement, a policing literalized in _The Matrix_’s AI agents, _Love Lies Bleeding’_s corrupt law enforcement, and Polo forcing Tara to drink the “luna juice”– quite literally drinking the kool-aid– trapping her in the midnight realm. 
![](@assets/blog/breaking-the-script/image12.png)This exhaustive maintenance inevitably falters– despite the constant normative noise of the midnight realm, there will always be “glitches in the matrix”, moments of _breaking_ with ordained reality. 
![](@assets/blog/breaking-the-script/image22.png)In _T.V. Glow_, these glitches– diegetic neon elements– leak into the midnight realm from the pink opaque, be it pink smoke from an ice cream van, or a literal crack in reality on Owen’s drive home.   

Even still, the constant friction between our bodies and these norms is incredibly damaging, serving as a continuous, “repetitive strain injury” that “contort[s]” and “twist[s]” our bodies into shapes unrecognizable to ourselves (Ahmed 145). 
![](@assets/blog/breaking-the-script/image4.png)In _The Matrix_, AI agents seal Neo’s mouth shut, “reprimand[ing]” him for daring to violate a regime that tells him “what to consume, where to go, whom to love”– in brief, how to live (Bey 137). As Awkward-Rich describes, coping with such violence often necessitates dissociation, harmfully “splitting” one’s body and mind from their self (Awkward-Rich 98). 
![](@assets/blog/breaking-the-script/image7.png)These dissociative aesthetics are incredibly palpable in _I Saw The TV Glow_, formalized through claustrophobic framing as Owen lies in the backseat, colored lights pulsating across her face, situating us in her detachment.  This is furthered by increasingly long temporal ellipses, skipping over large swathes of her life. Without the “supports to persist in [living]”, with her desires "unintelligible" to her mother and “condemned” by her father, Owen’s life is rendered “unlivable” (Malatino 111). 
![](@assets/blog/breaking-the-script/image20.png)This manifests itself literally, with Owen coughing up dirt, as her true identity– Isabel, buried alive in the midnight realm– is unable to break free. 
![](@assets/blog/breaking-the-script/image2.png)This existence is “unbearable”, and causes Owen to “break”, screaming “This isn’t my home”, vomiting sparks from the _Pink Opaque_ as her father washes off her nonconformity.  In _Love Lies Bleeding_, Jacky, too, suffers from a psychic break, thrusting us into a hallucinatory sequence as she overdoses in the muscle show. 
![](@assets/blog/breaking-the-script/image14.png)Unable to sustain her idealized masculine identity, Jacky is driven into madness, switching “sides”, her new identity serving as another prison and coercing her into “displacing” her anger horizontally at Lou (Butler 576; Malatino 114). This “break” exposes an increasingly widespread “somatopolitical condition” as marginalized subjects are increasingly crushed by the “necropolitical management” of modernity (Preciado 24). 
![](@assets/blog/breaking-the-script/image19.png)This necropolitical violence is personified by Lou Sr; smash cuts jump to his past, drowning him in blood-red colouring, with a harrowing demonic grin. There is a palpable horror here, foreshadowing Lou’s entanglement with these violent systems. Indeed, such entanglement is inevitable in modernity, where the “soul” itself is “dispersed in devices and institutions” and bound to “architectures and cables, machines and algorithms” (Preciado 16). 
![](@assets/blog/breaking-the-script/image16.png)In _The Matrix,_ Neo wakes up in the real world to find himself a monstrous cyborg, literally plugged into the machine of his subjugation. To break from this world, our protagonists (try to) embrace the “call to become monstrous”, rejecting the “logics of embodiment” that make their “[lives] unlivable” (Malatino 124). 
![](@assets/blog/breaking-the-script/image5.png)Extreme close-ups of unnaturally morphing muscles, coupled with the squelching sound of liquid interiority give Jacky a sense of identity in motion, a queerness that is “becoming” as she struggles to transition beyond the bounds of normative gender (Malatino 124).   

This call to monstrosity is deeply subversive, allowing for a beauty in refusing prescribed norms, opening up “imaginative possibilities not yet dreamed” of (Bey 144). These abrupt violations of norms, while unsettling, are productive; challenging respectability, they result in “affective transfer[s]” that “[enable] transformative worldmaking” (Ahmed 155; Malatino 116).  In Edelman’s words, queer forms “must insist on disturbing” and “queering” social norms as much as possible, to “[dissolve] those congealments of identity” that restrict our vision of ourselves (Edelman 17). To this end, the films in this program enact these disturbances formally, eroding our trust in absolute cinematic reality. _Love Lies Bleeding_ queers norms in its constant transition between genres: incorporating elements of romantic drama, horror, crime thriller, and a dark comedy, it never fully settles into one or the other. Instead, the film is constantly shifting and trans-itioning– reflecting trans-ness as a process and a force through time, characterized by “unpredictable flows across discrete forms” (Keegan 3). 
![](@assets/blog/breaking-the-script/image11.png)_I Saw the TV Glow_ achieves a similar destabilization by inverting its aspect ratios: flipping the widescreen ratio of the “real” world and the 4:3 ratio of the pink opaque, revealing the pink opaque to be the real world, and the “real world” to be the midnight realm. _The Matrix_ is perhaps the most violent in its ripping us from cinematic convention. A fast, mobile camera warps us into the world of the code, before smoothly transitioning back out into the flashlight of a police officer, immediately grounding the oppression of the matrix to that of our real-world police state. 
![](@assets/blog/breaking-the-script/image15.png)The ensuing confrontation entirely violates rules of space-time, slowing time as Trinity jumps and rotating around her in 360 degrees– a cinematic feat that “should” be impossible. As life within heteronorms becomes unlivable, this destabilization escalates into our characters “breaking” from their scripts. 
![](@assets/blog/breaking-the-script/image18.png)Neo is doubled as he makes the choice to break free from digital “reality”, just as Owen tries to shove her head in the TV, seeking to escape the “reality” of the midnight realm into the pink opaque. These scenes of rupture are explosive and emancipatory. 
![](@assets/blog/breaking-the-script/image13.png)Owen chuckles as she cuts herself open, revealing a hidden world of beaming neon lights within her, and Neo completely breaks the system, stopping bullets and literally exploding the agents of its enforcement.  
![](@assets/blog/breaking-the-script/image1.png)_Love Lies Bleeding_ epitomizes its transformative genre bending in a fantastical climax, showing Jacky and Lou as giants, running free in the clouds, before ending in an inversion of the crime thriller ending, showing us a future that is–uncharacteristically for a crime thriller– utopically free from the violent heteropatriarchal conditions of their prior existence and upbringing.  

Ultimately, these emancipatory moments of rupture, however ephemeral, highlight the transformational potential of rejecting normative scripts that demand we stay “confined within [their] bounds” (Bey 134). In _I Saw The TV Glow_, we see the suffocating, “coercive and compulsory” nature of these scripts, acting as “carceral logics” that bury us alive (Bey 137). _The Matrix_, on the other hand, shows the potential to subversively rewrite reality, marking the “capacity” for trans aesthetics to “transform one reality into another” (Keegan 3). Finally, _Love Lies Bleeding_ imparts the messy, material reality, where rage and the act of breaking embrace the “gloriousness of how things fall apart”, allowing queer subjects to pick up the “pieces” and “[drive] into the sunset” (Bey 135). In making visible the damaging, constructed, and fragile nature of heteronormative scripts, the films in this program urge us to break them; not to singlehandedly dismantle systems of oppression, but to begin the continuous process of “shapeshifting” and “taking shape” (Fawaz 366). As Keegan describes, transness is not an identification, but a “force [...] animating vitality itself” (Keegan 3). Together, these films show the vital necessity of breaking from the script, to cause a “SYSTEM FAILURE”, because “there _is_ still time” to break free, and drive off into a queer “world without borders and boundaries”.
