Queer people have long been relegated to the margins of historical society, bound to secret and often ephemeral moments of subversive lovemaking. Celine Sciamma's Portrait de la jeune fille en feu shows us a prime example of one such romance; the film follows Marianne, a painter, as she falls in love with Héloise, a young woman living on a remote island, soon-to-be-married to the suitor of her deceased sister. Beginning after the events of the main story have concluded, _Portrait_ is structured as a frame story from Marianne’s memory. Sciamma “plunge[s] us into [Marianne’s] subjective state” as the film explores her secret queer romance– hidden in the margins of historical French society– through her lingering, sensory impressions (Bordwell et al. 90). Spurred on by the sight of the titular portrait, we enter this memory with the knowledge of this romance’s impermanence, dripping in feelings of longing and nostalgia. Employing a distinctly painterly brush, Sciamma and cinematographer Claire Mathon portray these memories with strong perspective, vibrant colours, and diegetic sounds. Monochromatic outfits portray constrained social roles, textured sounds evoke a tactile, sensorial nature, whilst repeated perspective shots subvert the concept of the gaze. Through a close formal analysis of _Portrait_, this essay will demonstrate how these elements– framing, colour, and sound– formalize and communicate themes and emotions of queer unity and hidden worldmaking in the face of normative social constraints.

Throughout _Portrait_, the camera grounds us in Marianne’s perspective and subverts the concept of the gaze. As _Portrait_’s main story begins on a remote island in Brittany, long tracking shots frame Marianne frontally as she's led throughout the house, immediately immersing us in her perspective. From then on, the camera repeatedly mirrors Marianne’s eyes. 
![](@assets/blog/subversive-worldmaking/image6.png)When Marianne is shown to her room, the camera pans, following her gaze as she watches Sophie kindle the fire. When Sophie returns to fetch her the next morning, she is centrally framed, directly matching Marianne’s eyeline. Much of the early film emphasizes Marianne’s role as a painter and observer, “deform[ing]” reality into centrally framed “pictorial” compositions of her perspective (Bazin 27). This deforming gaze is formalized through a graphic match, joining two shots “to create a strong similarity of compositional elements” (Bordwell et al. G-2). 
![](@assets/blog/subversive-worldmaking/image12.png)The camera pans down to Héloise’s hands, before cutting to Marianne drawing them in the exact same pose. These moments don’t just formalize Marianne’s active status as an observer, they also imply a passivity of her portrayed subject, a concept that is flipped on its head as the film develops. The two-shot of the piano scene is a notable instance of this, frontally framing our central characters next to each other as they match each other’s gaze, revealing Héloise not just as a subject to be observed, but an observer in her own right. 
![](@assets/blog/subversive-worldmaking/image9.png)Subsequently, many of Marianne’s– and, through her gaze, the audience’s– assumptions about Héloise are proven wrong. For indeed, as Sontag argues, “interpretation” often amounts to a “refusal” to let one’s subject be, as we seek to avoid the discomfort “real art” might make us feel. Marianne’s rendering of Héloise as passive observer is as inaccurate as her first painting of her: a failed attempt to tame her, make her “manageable, conformable” (Sontag 8). The subversion of this docilizing gaze culminates when Héloise counters Marianne’s smug reading of her mannerisms, asserting they are in “exactly the same place”. 
![](@assets/blog/subversive-worldmaking/image2.png)When Marianne returns to continue painting, the camera jarringly matches Héloise’s eyeline, revealing an uncomfortable Marianne and codifying the reciprocal nature of their gaze. This reverse shot serves as a visual collision, and from here on the gaze shifts, with Héloise’s perspective interspersed with Marianne’s, formalizing her active participancy in her own story (Eisenstein 49).  As queer theorist Sara Ahmed writes, this discomfort is productive, and enables the central pairing to challenge the heteronormative scripts that seek to relegate them into passivity (Ahmed 149). It’s in this state, under equal standing, that the film thrusts us into their romance.
![](@assets/blog/subversive-worldmaking/image13.png)One scene in particular makes legible the intimacy and profundity of their connection, showing an extreme close-up as they kiss, their two lips unable to “part to let one word pass”, formalizing a unity and symmetry as they literally exchange saliva (Irigiray & Burke 72). This is further codified in Marianne’s parting gift to Héloise, as she draws herself in the blank space on the 28th page of the tragedy of Orpheus and Eurydice. 
![](@assets/blog/subversive-worldmaking/image7.png)Here, we see a frame-within-a-frame positioning Marianne’s face in front of Héloise’s lap, merging the two women into a single, composite entity– their “body shared” and “undivided”, sharply critiquing the heteronormative dynamic where the woman is but a passive mirror, barely alive to reflect the man’s gaze (Irigiray & Burke 71).

Colour serves many roles in _Portrait_, from communicating nostalgia and memory, helping establish its painterly aesthetics, as well as representing character constraints and roles. 
![](@assets/blog/subversive-worldmaking/image16.png)In the opening scene, colour grading and mise-en-scène create a cold, blue-dominated palette, evoking an “emotional colouration” of sadness and melancholy (Bordwell et al. 142). A visibly distraught Marianne is taken aback seeing la _Portrait d’une jeune fille en feu_, before we are thrust into her memory. This palette changes drastically as we do so, shifting to a warmer, vibrant hue. 
![](@assets/blog/subversive-worldmaking/image11.png)Even the night scenes are drenched in a warm glow from natural candle and fire light, communicating to us a sense of warm, romantic nostalgia and fond memory. The warm vibrancy of _Portrait_’s colours form a distinctly painterly aesthetic, another formal mirroring of its central character’s gaze. In André Bazin’s words, where some directors “put their faith in reality”, here Sciamma chooses to reflect Marianne’s  “faith in the image” and its “plastics”, grounding us in her memory and perspective (Bazin 24). Beyond the film’s overall colour palette, the role of colour is at its most prominent in the costuming of its key characters, formalizing their emotions and prescribed social roles. 
![](@assets/blog/subversive-worldmaking/image1.png)The red of Marianne’s dress brings up images of fire, agency, and passion– the same fire that will engulf Héloise. Héloise, for her part, has refused the emerald green dress, wearing instead a dark, somber blue, formalizing her mourning and protestful resignation to her dead sister’s fate. In Eisenstein’s terms, this visual opposition constitutes a “graphic conflict”, formalizing the friction arising from Marianne’s agency in her role as a painter, and Héloise’s lack thereof (Eisenstein 54). The colours also establish the costume pieces as visual motifs. 
![](@assets/blog/subversive-worldmaking/image8.png)With the audience understanding the green dress to be indicative of a prescribed role being thrust upon Héloise, Marianne putting on the dress formalizes her stepping into Héloise’s shoes and perspective, an early act of connection before the blossoming of their romance. 
![](@assets/blog/subversive-worldmaking/image15.png)Sciamma also subverts colour associations; in Marianne’s spectral visions, Héloise’s white dress creates a stark graphic conflict against the vacuous black of the background, rendering the visions an ominous omen of the inevitable future neither of them can escape. Graphic conflict also visually communicates inner conflict, coming to a head in the transformational bonfire scene: Héloise’s dark blue dress blends her into the shadowy background, situating her at ease in a solidaric community of women. 
![](@assets/blog/subversive-worldmaking/image17.png)The sudden, bright fire blazing the dress provides a strong contrast against the darkness, making visible the uncomfortable “queer feelings” catching fire between the two women. Rather than shrinking away or exuding panic, the pair remain transfixed, silently embracing an “excitement in the face of [...] uncertainty” as their inner desires seek to subvert the normative scripts that dictate their lives (Ahmed 155).

Whilst the use of colour provides bold contrasts, emphasizing the constraining nature of heteronormative scripts, Sciamma uses sound to strip away cinematic artifice and ground us in the sensorial intimacy of Marianne and Héloise’s connection. 
![](@assets/blog/subversive-worldmaking/image5.png)This is particularly prominent when Marianne rushes to the sea to apologise to Héloise. Whilst the pair cry and embrace, we are presented with no swooping score, just the crashing of the tide, flowing as freely as their tears, threatening to sweep their love away into the past, as they are forced to accept the ephemeral nature of their romance. The lack of score is consistent throughout almost the entire film, as Sciamma instead prioritizes textured, diegetic sounds, drawing us into the physical memories Marianne is attached to. Without a traditional score, our senses are heightened to the character’s breathing and footsteps, the rubbing of crayon and paint on canvas, and the crackling of the fireplace. The film’s sonic elements provide a “complete harmony of image and sound”, adopting a sort of poetic realism and embracing the “reality” that sound introduces to film (Bazin 29). This sonic texture grants the film an erotic and sensorial tactility. 
![](@assets/blog/subversive-worldmaking/image10.png)For instance, when taking flying ointment together, the texture of Marianne rubbing the hallucinogenic paste under Héloise’s armpit, combined with a close-up of said action renders their intimacy intensely palpable. The only real piece of score in the film is a choir piece composed for the bonfire scene– the same transformational moment where Héloise and Marianne’s romance bursts aflame. 
![](@assets/blog/subversive-worldmaking/image4.png)The music’s presence serves as yet another contrast, a clash with what we’ve come to expect in the film so far, catching our attention and creating new meaning as the ensemble nature of the piece evokes sentiments of community, unity, and solidarity. The absence of score throughout the rest of this film makes its presence here that much more impactful, and allows any music that _is_ present to serve as an immediately identifiable motif. Such is the case with Vivaldi’s four seasons, a piece Marianne plays for Héloise as they first fall in love, that returns in spectacular, heart-wrenching fashion in the film’s final moments– a quintessential example of music’s ability to “dominate [...] emotion-laden moments without dialogue”– as Marianne observes Héloise one more time (Bordwell et al. 272).

In these final moments, _Portrait’s_ formal elements and their functions all join as one. The camera pushes in to observe Héloise listening to the opera for the first time, as we and Marianne get to gaze upon her love for the last. 
![](@assets/blog/subversive-worldmaking/image3.png)The colours are warm and soft, as if she was lit by flame, for indeed in looking at the orchestra, she too is really looking at Héloise, even if their gazes never meet. And the orchestra is taking flight, as the flame of their love burns ever still. It is here we see the crucial pertinence of these specific elements: framing, colour and sound. Together, they draw a clear picture of the liminal time and space queer romances are forced to reside in. Framing subverts the (male) gaze by grounding it in a female viewpoint, which it varies to provide agency to her subject. Colour reinforces the warmth of their connection, whilst further illustrating the social scripts they must inhabit; even Marianne’s seeming independence is contingent on ties to her father: she can’t even paint under her own name. Diegetic sound draws us into the sensory experience of her memories, providing a tactility grounding their connection in physical reality, whilst the sparing use of music provides a cathartic release from the social scripts that bind them.  
![](@assets/blog/subversive-worldmaking/image14.png)Yes, their material conditions have forced them to “become two”, and “on the outside” they “conform to an order” which alienates them, yet beyond this inscrutable exterior lies an inner world of queer making (Irigiray & Burke 73). One which– answering Sontag’s call– lives on in the erotics of Marianne’s art, etched into subversive permanence on the 28th page of the tragedy of Orpheus and Eurydice (Sontag 14).
